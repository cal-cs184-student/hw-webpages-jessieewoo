OVERVIEW — 3 pts

In this assignment, I built a real-time cloth simulation from scratch, covering everything from the physical structure of the cloth to how it looks on screen. In Part 1, I constructed the cloth as a grid of point masses connected by three different types of springs — structural, shearing, and bending — each responsible for a different aspect of how real fabric behaves. This gave the cloth its fundamental shape and resistance to deformation.

In Parts 2 through 4, I focused on the physics simulation itself. Part 2 used Verlet integration to update the cloth's position each frame based on forces like gravity and spring tension, with a per-step constraint to prevent springs from overstretching. Part 3 added collision handling with external objects — specifically a sphere and a plane — by detecting when a point mass crosses a surface and pushing it back to the correct side. Part 4 tackled self-collision, which is where the cloth needs to avoid passing through itself. I implemented spatial hashing to efficiently find nearby point masses and apply correction forces when they get too close, which was a lot more interesting than I expected.

Finally, Part 5 was all about making the cloth look good with GLSL shaders. I implemented Blinn-Phong lighting, texture mapping, bump mapping, displacement mapping, and a mirror shader. The bump vs. displacement comparison was especially cool — they look almost the same in photos but are fundamentally different under the hood. Overall this project was a great tour of how real-time cloth simulation actually works at a technical level.

PART 1 — 17 pts

Masses and Springs

Walk through how you built the cloth grid.

The cloth grid is built in Cloth::buildGrid() in src/cloth.cpp in two phases: first create all the point masses, then add the springs between them.

For the point masses, I iterate in row-major order (y outer loop, x inner loop). If the cloth is horizontal, all point masses get a fixed y = 1 and their positions vary over the xz-plane. If it's vertical, positions vary over the xy-plane with a tiny random z-offset between -1/1000 and 1/1000 to prevent the simulation from going degenerate (without that offset, perfectly flat vertical cloth can behave weirdly). Each point mass also checks if its (x, y) index appears in the pinned list from the JSON config and marks itself as pinned if so.

Then for springs, I loop over every point mass and add up to 6 springs depending on its neighbors:

  • Structural: connects to the immediate left neighbor and the one directly above. These are the backbone of the grid — they keep the cloth from falling apart along the horizontal and vertical axes.
  • Shearing: connects diagonally to the upper-left and upper-right neighbors. These resist shear deformation — without them, the cloth would collapse into a parallelogram shape when forces are applied diagonally.
  • Bending: connects to the point mass two steps to the left and two steps above (skipping the immediate neighbor). Unlike structural springs, bending springs model the stiffness of real fabric against folding and sharp creases. They use 0.2× the normal spring constant since they're meant to be softer — see simulate() line: double ks_used = (s.spring_type == BENDING) ? cp->ks * 0.2 : cp->ks;

PART 2 — 20 pts

Simulation via Numerical Integration

How does the simulation step work?

All of this lives in Cloth::simulate() in src/cloth.cpp. Each timestep has three main stages:

  • Force accumulation: Start by computing the total external force on each point mass (just gravity times mass for now). Then loop over all springs and apply Hooke's law: F = ks * (‖b - a‖ − rest_length) * (b-a)/‖b-a‖. Bending springs use 0.2× ks so they're gentler. Forces are added to pm_a and subtracted from pm_b.
  • Verlet integration: For each non-pinned point mass: new_pos = pos + (1 - d) * (pos - last_pos) + (F/m) * dt² where d is the damping coefficient (between 0 and 1). The (1-d) factor on the velocity term is what provides damping — it slows things down without needing to explicitly track velocity.
  • Provot stretch constraint [1995]: After integration, go through every spring and if its current length exceeds 1.1× its rest length, correct the positions of its two endpoint masses to bring it back within range. If one endpoint is pinned, only move the free one; otherwise split the correction 50/50.

Effect of the spring constant ks

ks controls how stiff the springs are, which has a huge effect on how the cloth behaves:

  • Very low ks (≈50 N/m): the cloth behaves almost like a liquid — it stretches dramatically under gravity, sags deeply between the two pinned corners, and oscillates for a long time before settling. The surface looks very loose and rubbery, with large, smooth folds.
  • High ks (≈50000 N/m): the cloth behaves much more like a stiff sheet of paper or metal. It barely stretches, falls quickly with very little sag, and snaps into its resting position almost immediately with minimal oscillation. The surface appears taut and flat between the pinned points.

Effect of density

Density controls how heavy the cloth is, which changes how much gravitational force each point mass experiences (since mass = width * height * density / num_points):

  • Low density (1 g/cm²): the cloth is very lightweight and falls slowly, almost floating down before settling. The final resting shape has a shallow sag between the two pinned corners, and the cloth barely droops.
  • High density (1500 g/cm²): the cloth is heavy and falls fast, pulling downward aggressively. The final resting shape has a very deep, pronounced droop — the middle of the cloth hangs much lower than the pinned corners. It has a similar visual effect to low ks, but the underlying cause is different: here the springs are the same stiffness, but the gravitational force is way larger.

density = 5 g/cm² — light cloth, shallow sag

density = 500 g/cm² — heavy cloth, deep droop

Effect of damping

Damping controls how quickly the cloth loses energy — it's applied as a multiplier on the velocity term in Verlet integration: (1 - d) * (pos - last_pos).

  • Very low damping (≈0.05%): the cloth oscillates wildly for a long time — it swings past its resting position, bounces back, and keeps going, simulating an almost frictionless, energy-conserving system. It eventually settles but takes many seconds.
  • Very high damping (≈1.0%): the cloth falls in slow motion, barely swinging at all, and settles immediately into its resting position without any oscillation. It almost looks like the cloth is moving through thick syrup. The difference is most dramatic right at the start of the simulation when motion is greatest.

damping ≈ 0% — cloth swinging wildly past rest position

damping ≈ 100% — cloth barely moving, settles immediately

Final resting state with scene/pinned4.json (default parameters)

With default parameters (ks = 5000 N/m, density = 15 g/cm², damping = 0.2%), the cloth in pinned4.json comes to rest as a smooth, gently bowed surface with all four corners pinned. Gravity pulls the center of the cloth downward, creating a symmetrical concave dip across the whole sheet. The edges remain relatively taut between the corner pins, and the overall shape resembles a shallow hammock or a sail under light wind.

PART 3 — 20 pts

Handling Collisions with Other Objects

How sphere and plane collisions work

After each Verlet integration step, every point mass is tested against every collision object. Here's how each one is handled:

  • Sphere collisions (src/collision/sphere.cpp:Sphere::collide()): I check whether a point mass has entered the sphere by comparing the squared distance from the point mass to the sphere's origin against the squared radius: (pm.position - origin).norm2() < radius2. If it's inside, I compute the tangent point — the point on the sphere's surface along the ray from the origin through the point mass's current position: tangent = origin + (pm.position - origin).unit() * radius. Then I compute the correction vector from last_position to that tangent point, and set the new position to last_position + correction * (1 - friction), which simulates the cloth sliding along the surface.
  • Plane collisions (src/collision/plane.cpp:Plane::collide()): I detect a crossing by computing the signed distances of the current and last positions relative to the plane: curr_side = dot(pm.position - point, normal) and same for last_position. If the point is on the wrong side of the plane (i.e., curr_side < 0), I compute the exact tangent point along the path between the two positions using linear interpolation. The point mass is then bumped slightly above the tangent point by a small SURFACE_OFFSET = 0.0001 constant on the side it originated from, and the new position is set to last_position + correction * (1 - friction). There's also a fallback for the case where Provot's constraint has already pushed a point mass through the plane in one step — in that case we just project it directly onto the plane.

  • ks = 500: the cloth is much more flexible and conforms tightly to the sphere's shape. The top surface is very smooth and closely hugs the sphere, while the sides hang lower with softer, more numerous folds. The cloth almost looks like a wet piece of fabric clinging to the surface.

PART 4 — 20 pts

Handling Self-Collisions

How self-collision detection works

The naive approach of checking every pair of point masses for proximity would be O(n²) per timestep — way too slow for any reasonable cloth resolution. Instead, I used spatial hashing to get this down to roughly O(n) average case.

The implementation is spread across three functions in src/cloth.cpp:

  • hash_position(): hashes a 3D position into a unique float key representing membership in a 3D box. The box dimensions are w = 3 * width / num_width_points, h = 3 * height / num_height_points, t = max(w, h). I floor each coordinate divided by its box dimension to get integer box coordinates, then combine them with large prime multipliers (31, 97, 101) to reduce hash collisions: bx * 31 + by * 31 * 97 + bz * 31 * 97 * 101.
  • build_spatial_map(): called once per timestep. Clears the old hashmap, then loops over all point masses and inserts each one into the bucket matching its hashed position. Point masses in the same 3D box end up in the same bucket.
  • self_collide(): for a given point mass, looks up its hash bucket and checks every other point mass in that bucket. If a candidate is within 2 * thickness distance and isn't the point mass itself, compute a correction vector pushing the two apart to restore the 2 * thickness separation. All pairwise corrections are averaged together, then scaled down by simulation_steps to keep position changes gradual and stable.

In simulate(), build_spatial_map() is called once per timestep, followed by self_collide() on every point mass. This runs before the external collision checks.

Density changes how heavy the cloth is, which affects both how fast it falls and how hard it hits itself:

  • Low density (1 g/cm²): the cloth is light and falls slowly, creating large, gradual folds as it lands on itself. The folds are smooth and spread out — the cloth kind of floats down and layers gently.

PART 5 — 20 pts

Shaders

What is a shader program? How do vertex and fragment shaders work together?

A shader program is code that runs directly on the GPU to control the rendering pipeline. There are two main types that work together in sequence:

  • Vertex shader (*.vert): runs once per vertex of the mesh. Its main job is transforming 3D positions in model space into 2D screen coordinates using the model, view, and projection matrices (gl_Position = u_view_projection * u_model * position). It also passes data along to the fragment shader — things like UV coordinates, normals, and tangent vectors — which get automatically interpolated across the triangle's surface by the rasterizer.
  • Fragment shader (*.frag): runs once per pixel (fragment) after rasterization. It takes the interpolated values from the vertex shader and computes the final color for that pixel — sampling textures, computing lighting, applying bump offsets, etc. This is where all the visual magic happens.

The pipeline is: vertex shader → rasterization (GPU interpolates vertex data across each triangle) → fragment shader → final image. The vertex shader handles geometry, the fragment shader handles appearance.

Blinn-Phong shading model

Blinn-Phong splits lighting into three independent components that get added together. The full equation from shaders/Phong.frag is: 

L = ka * Ia  +  kd * (I/r²) * max(0, n·l)  +  ks * (I/r²) * max(0, n·h)^p
  • Ambient (ka * Ia): a constant base illumination applied uniformly everywhere. Prevents surfaces from going completely black even in shadow. In the shader, ka = vec3(0.1) and Ia = vec3(1.0).
  • Diffuse (kd * (I/r²) * max(0, n·l)): light that scatters equally in all directions from the surface. Depends on the angle between the surface normal n and the direction to the light l. The I/r² factor accounts for light falling off with distance squared.
  • Specular (ks * (I/r²) * max(0, n·h)^p): the shiny highlight. Uses the halfway vector h = normalize(l + v) (between the light direction and view direction) instead of computing a full reflection. The exponent p = 64.0 controls the tightness of the highlight — larger p means a smaller, sharper, shinier-looking spot.

The Blinn-Phong key insight is using h instead of a reflection vector, which is computationally cheaper and avoids edge-case artifacts at grazing angles.

Bump mapping vs. displacement mapping

Both shaders use the same height map (texture_2.png or a custom one) to create the illusion of surface detail, but they work very differently:

  • Bump mapping (shaders/Bump.frag): the geometry doesn't actually change. Instead, the fragment shader perturbs the surface normal using finite differences of the height map. It builds a TBN (tangent-bitangent-normal) matrix from the vertex's local frame, computes the height gradient dU = (h(uv + [1/w, 0]) - h(uv)) * k_h * k_n and dV similarly, constructs a perturbed tangent-space normal n0 = (-dU, -dV, 1.0), transforms it to world space via TBN, and uses that for Blinn-Phong lighting. The silhouette of the object looks exactly the same — only the shading changes.
  • Displacement mapping (shaders/Displacement.vert + shaders/Displacement.frag): the vertex shader physically moves each vertex along its model-space normal by the height value: displaced = in_position + in_normal * h(uv) * u_height_scaling. This actually changes the geometry, so silhouettes and edges show real bumps. The fragment shader then applies the same bump-normal perturbation as before for fine lighting detail.

Mirror shader

The mirror shader (shaders/Mirror.frag) creates a perfect reflective surface by computing the reflection of the incoming view ray across the surface normal, then sampling the environment cubemap in that direction: 

vec3 wo = normalize(v_position.xyz - u_cam_pos);
vec3 wi = reflect(wo, n);
out_color = texture(u_texture_cubemap, wi);

Since wo is the direction from the camera to the surface, reflecting it across n gives the direction that a perfect mirror would send the ray — and then we just look up what the environment looks like in that direction. The result is a cloth and sphere that look like polished chrome, reflecting the scene around them.
